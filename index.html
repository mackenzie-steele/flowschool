The energy of the room
The part nobody taught you

Teacher training gave you poses. Then it gave you a Tuesday night class.

You know how to move. Nobody showed you how to keep creating week after week without burning out. It usually looks like this:

  • Planning one class takes your whole afternoon.
  • Every week feels like starting from a blank page.
  • Your best ideas live in six different notes apps.
  • You teach sequences that don't sound like you.
  • You stopped experimenting somewhere along the way.

You're not a bad teacher. You just never got a system. That's what this is.

What Flow School is

A method for building classes, and a studio to practice it in.

Bonnie Weeks teaches yoga teachers. Her whole method — how to sequence, how to cue, how to sound like yourself instead of a script — lives here, as videos you can learn from and tools you can practice with.

You watch a class get built. Then you build your own, teach it, and come back next week to make it better. That's the whole idea.

The tools

Software built around how teaching actually works.

Write classes you can actually teach from.

Plan in shorthand — quick to write, easy to remember, readable from the mat in the middle of class. Every class you write stays in your library, so you can teach it again next month and make it a little better each time.

Playlist Builder

Shape the energy. Then build the music to match.

Draw the arc of your class — where it builds, where it peaks, where it lands — and build a playlist that follows the same shape. Music moves the room as much as the sequence does. It deserves more than shuffle.

Never start from a blank page again.

Constraints, movement experiments, story prompts — small starting points that get you on the mat before you open a plan. You figure out what you want to teach by moving through it. After that, writing the class is the easy part.

Practice cueing until clear language is second nature.

Every clear cue is a verb, a body part, and a direction. Build yours, cut the extra words, and practice them at the speed of real breath — until people stop looking around the room to copy someone else and start following you.
